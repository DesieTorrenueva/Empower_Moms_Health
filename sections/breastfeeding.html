Breastfeeding Support

Breastfeeding support during pregnancy and postpartum focuses on education, early skin-to-skin contact, frequent feeding, proper latch techniques, and ongoing guidance from healthcare providers and support systems. This support helps mothers establish successful breastfeeding and manage common challenges.

Breastfeeding Support During Pregnancy

Breastfeeding support begins during pregnancy through education and planning. Learning early helps build confidence and prepares mothers for successful nursing.

  • Education: Learn about breastfeeding benefits, techniques, and challenges through classes, books, or trusted websites.
  • Planning: Create a postpartum plan that includes feeding, recovery, and emotional support.
  • Preparation: Discuss breastfeeding goals and self-care with your healthcare provider.
  • Counseling: Healthcare providers can address concerns, including breastfeeding while pregnant, which is usually safe in healthy pregnancies.

Immediately After Birth (First Hours & Days)

  • Skin-to-Skin Contact: Place the baby on the mother’s chest immediately to encourage bonding and feeding.
  • Early Initiation: Encourage breastfeeding as soon as possible after birth.
  • Rooming-In: Keep mother and baby together 24 hours a day to promote frequent feeding.
  • No Supplements: Avoid giving newborns other fluids or foods unless medically necessary.

Postpartum Care & Common Challenges

  • Feeding on Demand: Breastfeed at least 8 times in 24 hours, responding to baby cues such as rooting or hand sucking.
  • Latch & Positioning: Ensure the baby has a wide mouth and covers most of the areola for effective feeding.
  • Engorgement: Use warm compresses before feeding and cold compresses after feeding; feed frequently.
  • Afterpains: Mild uterine contractions during breastfeeding are normal; pain relievers may help.
  • Nutrition: Consume an extra 450–500 calories per day, drink plenty of fluids, and choose nutritious foods.
  • Professional Support: Seek help from lactation consultants (IBCLCs), nurses, or midwives if problems persist.

Key Breastfeeding Support Systems

  • Healthcare Providers: Nurses, doctors, midwives, and lactation consultants.
  • Family & Friends: Emotional encouragement and practical help.
  • Support Organizations: Breastfeeding groups and community resources.

Importance of Breastfeeding

Protecting, promoting, and supporting breastfeeding saves more lives than any other preventive intervention. Exclusive and continued breastfeeding can prevent up to 13% of deaths among children under five globally.

Breastfed children experience fewer infections, lower risk of chronic diseases, higher IQ scores, and improved long-term outcomes. Mothers also benefit from reduced health risks and financial savings.

World Health Organization (WHO) Recommendations

  • Initiate breastfeeding within one hour of birth
  • Provide exclusive breastfeeding for the first six months
  • Continue breastfeeding until at least two years of age
  • Introduce safe and adequate complementary foods at six months

WHO works with governments and organizations to protect breastfeeding from harmful marketing practices and promote maternity protection and baby-friendly healthcare systems.
